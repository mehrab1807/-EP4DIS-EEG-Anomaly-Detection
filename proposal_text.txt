Dissertation Project Title
An Experimental Comparison of Kalman Filtering and Auto-Regressive Time-Series Models (ARIMA, ARMAX) for Fault Detection in Epileptic EEG Data.
Research Problem & Rationale
Detecting the sudden and dramatic changes in nonstationary, complex biomedical signals (i.e. detecting when an epileptic seizure occurs in electroencephalograms [EEGs]) presents a daunting mathematical challenge. In environments that are relatively stable and periodic, traditional threshold-based anomaly detection models perform well; however, brain waves have a continuously changing random component and also contain very large amounts of observational random noise.  At present, most researchers use linear autoregressive models (including ARIMA) to create the predictive baseline for what should be considered normal. These single variable models have difficulty adapting to the fast, chaotic transitions of an ictal (seizure) event, resulting in unacceptable false positive rates due to normal physiological artifacts (e.g. eye blinks). In addition, these models do not account for how seizures propagate through space from one hemisphere of the brain to another.  There is a significant gap in literature in comparison of whether using external spatial information (ARMAX) or applying advanced recursive Bayesian state estimation (Kalman Filter) would allow better identification of the underlying hidden state of the brain.
Research Aim
The ultimate goal of this research is to test and compare the effectiveness of advanced Bayesian state estimation (Kalman Filter) versus traditional linear autoregressive models (ARIMA, ARMAX) for the purpose of real-time fault detection in nonstationary biomedical time series data.  This research aligns with current advanced Data Science and AI methodologies to solve the problem of creating reliable, automatic anomaly detection in highly volatile environments. Through solving the problems of traditional threshold-based methods, this research aims to prove if incorporating exogenous spatial variables or recursive state-estimation can accurately isolate true nonstationarity (seizure) from the stochastic noise of biological signals.  The potential impacts of this research will occur in three major areas. From a technical perspective, this research hopes to significantly decrease the rate of false positives and improve the latency of continuous fault detection systems. Economically and socially, this research will increase the reliability of algorithms in neurodiagnostic tools to optimize computational efficiencies and reduce unnecessary clinical interventions. Finally, academically, this research will fill a large knowledge gap by providing a clear, quantifiable benchmark using an open-access dataset. Ultimately, this research will provide an explainable anomaly detection framework that will advance the field of biomedical signal processing and can be easily applied to other high noise industrial applications, such as algorithmic finance modelling.

Preliminary Literature Review
4.1. Introduction: The Clinical and Mathematical Context of EEG Anomalies  As mentioned previously, in the area of biomedical signal processing, the task of developing mathematical approaches for identifying structural breaks in brain activity — specifically identifying the onset of epileptic seizures — is one of the largest challenges remaining. Epilepsy is defined by sudden transitions in the brain's dynamical state, transitioning from a resting, normative state (interictal) to a chaotic, hyper-synchronous state (ictal). Long-term ambulatory and/or 24-hour video EEG is currently the gold standard for measuring these transitions. However, manually reviewing long term recordings of these signals is extremely laborious and susceptible to human error. Therefore, there is a strong need for developing automated detection systems capable of rapidly identifying structural breaks in EEG signals (Baldin et al., 2024; Losey et al., 2024).  Additionally, the need for automation is increased by the fact that routine short term ancillary tests often fail to detect sporadic ictal events, thus necessitating longer term recording sessions where the sheer volume of continuous EEG data overwhelms manual diagnostic abilities (Burkholder et al. 2025).  Mathematically, the transition into a seizure can be viewed as a structural break in the established baseline of the patient’s brainwaves, occurring at the zone of neural fragility (Li et al. 2021). Although several methods have been developed for translating raw EEG data into clinically useful alerts, these require careful post-processing temporal sequences to prevent alert fatigue and mitigate the extreme stochastic drift that exists in biological signals (Pinto et al. 2024; Sriraam et al. 2024).  4.2. Classical Linear Baselines: AR, ARMA, and ARIMA Modelling  The use of Auto-Regressive (AR) based models to provide predictive thresholds for fault detection in biosignals have been explored extensively in recent years. Although the foundational AR model has a strong assumption that the present value of a signal can be linearly predicted from immediately previous values, hybrid models consisting of AR models paired with support vector machines (SVMs) have provided successful demonstrations of real-time prediction, however they have difficulty with the complex and non-stationary characteristics of human data (Sharma, R., et al., 2026). Contemporary research has therefore used the Auto-Regressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) model to address the issue of non-stationarity. The ARIMA model introduces an integrating term (differencing) to create a stationary base from which to predict the interictal brain state, effectively reducing stochastic drift (Nanda et al., 2024).   Although there have been significant advancements in the area of time-series classification using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Deep Learning for classification, the black box nature of many of these models limit their ability to provide the necessary mathematical transparency required in critical medical applications (Ayllón-Gavilán et al., 2023). Therefore, despite providing superior performance in terms of accuracy and speed, linear models such as ARIMA remain popular due to their explanatory capabilities (Guijo-Rubio et al., 2024). However, although linear models provide excellent performance for most scenarios, they do not possess the flexibility to quickly adapt to rapid changes in a highly chaotic ictal event. These rapid changes result in spikes in prediction errors. Despite these spikes acting as a theoretical trigger for a "fault", comparative studies have demonstrated that linear classifiers generate unacceptably high false positive rates and frequently confuse normal physiological artefacts (such as patient movement) with seizures (Alyas et al., 2023). Additionally, existing evaluation metrics often do not sufficiently penalize false positives resulting in a masking of the limitations of univariate methods in both human and animal automated detection trials (Xiong, 2024; Zhang et al., 2025).  4.3. Modelling Spatial Dynamics and Exogenous Stimuli (ARX and ARMAX)  One of the fundamental deficiencies of standard ARIMA modelling is its univariate nature. That is, it analyzes each channel individually and does not account for the interconnections within the brain. The brain is a highly interconnected network and a structural break in one neural region will often be preceded by localized anomalies in other regions. Advanced neural mass models emphasize the need for distributed delay architectures to model the spatial correlations within the brain (Smith et al., 2025). In traditional time-series forecasting, this spatial interconnection is modeled by including Exogenous Inputs, resulting in ARX and ARMAX architectures.  Recent literature has successfully utilized algorithms such as Particle Swarm Optimisation to improve the spectral estimation of EEG signals utilizing ARMAX frameworks (Koirala, 2023). By feeding the signal from one electrode as an exogenous 'X' input into the predictive equation of a neighboring electrode, ARMAX models can detect the propagation of a seizure significantly earlier than univariate baselines. Beyond detection, ARMAX provides a foundational basis for therapeutic intervention. As previously stated, exogenous inputs may be mathematically manipulated to simulate the application of a targeted electrical suppression and thereby allow researchers to model how an external control stimulus may cause the dynamic system to return to a normative interictal baseline (Silva et al., 2024).  4.4. Advanced Bayesian State-Tracking: The Kalman Filter  To overcome the limitations of linear models in high-noise environments, researchers are increasingly developing recursive Bayesian state-tracking algorithms. Due to the fact that phenomelogical model states are extremely difficult to estimate during the chaotic phase of a seizure, algorithms must rapidly determine if an observed anomaly is a true state transition or merely extreme background noise (Sharma, A., et al., 2025).  The Kalman Filter is currently the state-of-the-art solution to this particular mathematical problem. Unlike ARIMA models which attempt to directly predict the raw signal, the Kalman Filter uses a continuous two-step process of prediction and Bayesian updating to separate the "true" hidden state of the biological system from the stochastic observational noise. Recent studies demonstrating the success of implementing mixed filtering approaches on multi-channel EEG data, have shown that Kalman filtering provides superior efficacy at real-time artefact rejection (Rios et al., 2021). Advances in the area include the integration of tensor-network Kalman Filters with LS-SVMs showing potential in tracking continuous hidden state transitions during highly chaotic seizures (de Rooij et al., 2023). Further advancements, such as those achieved through the implementation of concatenated serial-parallel block techniques have resulted in a drastic reduction in the detection latency and false positive rates historically experienced in biosignal tracking (Al-Fahoum et al., 2024).  4.5. Gap Analysis and Synthesis  Although the theoretical efficacies of each of the three types of algorithms - ARIMA, ARMAX, and Kalman Filters - have been clearly documented, there has yet to be a direct, comprehensive experimental comparison of these algorithms on a unified, high-frequency dataset. Additionally, the literature highlights a critical need to narrow the gap between the application of traditional predictive methods and that of dynamic state-tracking. By establishing consistent evaluation metrics, and testing the aforementioned mathematical architectures on the same state transitions contained within the chosen dataset (ds003029), this research will provide a direct response to the limitations of real-time anomaly detection currently experienced in non-stationary domains.
Identify Research Gap
Current biosignal research commonly utilizes linear auto-regressive frameworks (ARIMA) to develop baseline predictive thresholds for electroencephalogram (EEG) data. A major knowledge deficit however, lies in how effective they are in real-world applications; these uni-variate models often do not effectively incorporate the extreme non-stationarity and spatial propagation associated with ictal state transitions leading to very high false positive rates caused by normal biological artifacts.  On the other hand, although advanced recursive Bayesian state-tracking technologies—specifically the Kalman Filter—are theoretically superior in separating true hidden states from random noise, current literature often tests these algorithms in isolation. Further, most literature relies on proprietary clinical datasets, thereby limiting academic reproducibility.  This research addresses this methodological void by creating a unified, rigorous comparison of uni-variate baselines (ARIMA), spatially integrated multi-variate models (ARMAX utilizing exogenous cross-channel inputs), and recursive Bayesian state-tracking (Kalman Filter). Through the benchmarking of these unique mathematical frameworks against a single dataset provided through the OpenNeuro open-access repository (Accession Number: ds003029), this project creates a highly reproducible Design of Experiments. This project will determine if including spatial dynamics or recursive noise-filtering can eliminate the practical issues of detection delay and accuracy found with classical linear models in automated fault detection.

Research Design and Methods
This study uses a quantitative, computational methodology based on the secondary analysis of pre-recorded human biological data. To allow for a rigorous, reproducible approach, the research design will be broken down into four sequential Work Packages (WPs): 5.1 WP1: Foundation & Preprocessing  After a systematic literature review of mathematical fault detection, the primary data will be obtained from the OpenNeuro open-access repository (Dataset Accession Number: ds003029). After obtaining the raw electroencephalogram (EEG) time series, the data will go through a rigorous preprocessing phase, which includes band-pass filtering and artefact removal to remove biological noise from the data. After removing artefacts from the data, the cleaned data will be chronologically epoched so that only the transition from the inter-ictal baseline to the ictal state will be captured. 5.2 WP2: Baseline Linear Modelling  Normative predictive thresholds will be established using the cleaned inter-ictal data segments. This will involve building univariate models (ARIMA) to model temporal dependencies in the data. Following this, spatially integrated multivariate models (ARMAX) will be developed to include exogenous variables across multiple EEG channels. 5.3 WP3: Advanced State-Tracking  This phase focuses on developing reliable architectures to detect anomalies from continuous stochastic drift. Recursive Bayesian state-space models will be built, and a particular emphasis will be placed on the rigorous calibration of the Kalman Filter covariance matrix. The goal will be to optimize the performance of the Kalman Filter to isolate hidden states and accurately track rapid changes in dynamics (ictal events) amid extreme observational noise. 5.4 WP4: Evaluation & Synthesis  A symmetrical Design of Experiments (DoE) will be performed to formally compare the predictive efficacy of the linear baselines (WP2) to those of the advanced state tracking models (WP3). Strict statistical metrics will be used to evaluate the algorithms including: Root Mean Square Error (RMSE); False Positive Rates (FPR); and computational detection latency. The results will then be synthesized to create the final dissertation manuscript.
Data Collection Strategy
The primary data source is the OpenNeuro open-access repository (Dataset Accession Number: ds003029). This is an open-access, secondary dataset containing anonymized, high-frequency human electroencephalogram (EEG) recordings. Importantly, the dataset contains clinically verified annotations for both normative (inter-ictal) brain activity and non-stationary deviations (ictal events). Since this is a purely computational study, no primary human data collection will take place.  The collection technique is to download the raw EEG files which are formatted according to the BIDS format for brain imaging data structure.  The initial data analysis techniques will strictly focus on signal preprocessing to create a clean reference. EEG signals are inherently contaminated by various forms of noise. Thus, after acquiring the raw EEG data, the continuous time-series signals will be filtered to extract the relevant neurological frequency bands using band-pass filtering, and subsequently, artefact rejection will be applied to mathematically remove the biological noise from the data (e.g., ocular movement). Finally, the cleaned data will be segmented chronologically (epoched) into specific temporal windows to perfectly isolate the transition from the inter-ictal baseline to the ictal state, preparing the data for the algorithmic modelling stage.

Ethical and Legal Considerations
The processing of human biological information is involved with this study and therefore it is important that the researcher adheres to the UK GDPR and data management protocols, even if the data being used is from a secondary source. An important professional concern is the possibility of algorithmic bias in clinical neurodiagnostic applications. Clinical explainability may also be impacted by the lack of transparency in the mathematical decision-making process. Additionally, the constant processing of large amounts of high frequency time series data will require large amounts of computational power, creating environmental concerns about the amount of energy consumed during processing.
Proposed Mitigation Measures
In order to protect the data collected from the participants and meet the requirements of the Data Use Agreement for the OpenNeuro repository, the data will be processed using the pre-anonymized data from the OpenNeuro repository. All analytical scripts will be stored on OneDrive under the password protected folder at Aston University. In addition, in order to minimize the professional risk of developing an AI model that produces biased results, the methodology utilises only mathematically transparent linear and Bayesian models, and excludes the utilization of any opaque deep learning architectures. Lastly, in order to minimize the environmental impact of the study, the analytical pipeline will utilize vectorized operations and efficient management of cloud cluster allocations to minimize unnecessary energy consumption due to computation.
